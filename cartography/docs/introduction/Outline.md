**Lead:** Effective fisheries management depends on knowing where fish are.
**Development:**
- Stock assessments of highly mobile species require understanding their spatial distribution and behavior.
- To understand where fish are, one must follow the journey — and for many highly mobile species, that journey begins with a migration.

---

**Lead:** Many highly mobile pelagic fish undertake vast, directed migrations toward areas of reliably high productivity.
**Development:**
- Migrations can be enormous — a whale shark traveling 7,213 km, bluefin tuna covering 111,883 km over four years.
- Navigation is achieved through magnetic maps, olfactory waypoints imprinted during juvenile migration, and social memory passed between generations.
- Critically, fish are heading not toward what is there now, but toward what tends to be there — prior knowledge of reliable productivity is embedded in the behavior.

---

**Lead:** Once at the foraging grounds, movement is not uniform — fish modulate their search behavior in response to local conditions, lingering where productivity is high.
**Development:**
- In prey-sparse, featureless waters fish adopt Lévy flights — clusters of small steps punctuated by long jumps — to efficiently sample new areas.
- In productive patches, they switch to intensive Brownian search, concentrating effort locally.
- The transition between these modes is driven by encounter with specific features.

---

**Lead:** The features that anchor both migration endpoints and search behavior — mesoscale eddies, thermal fronts, seamounts — constitute the marine landscape.
**Development:**
- These features aggregate prey biomass through distinct physical mechanisms.
- They are transient, appearing and disappearing over weeks to months, making the landscape fundamentally dynamic.
- Understanding fish distributions therefore requires characterizing this landscape.

---

**Lead:** This study will develop datasets characterizing the dynamic oceanographic features that constitute the marine landscape, providing a resource for downstream modeling of fish movement and distribution.
**Development:**
- Define what "landscape" means in a pelagic marine context, drawing on marine ecology.
- Identify how each landscape feature should be characterized — what variables and metrics best capture their presence, extent, and dynamics.
- Produce a dataset characterizing those features over time across the North Pacific.
- Identify stable regions — areas where specific landscape features recur consistently across years.
