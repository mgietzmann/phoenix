**Lead:** Sound travels far in water, and many predators are tuned to the signals of struggling prey.
**Development:**
- (Nelson, 1963) In field experiments, these predators ignore continuous tones, instead homing in on sounds that mimic the "thrumming" vibrations of a speared black grouper struggling against a reef
- (Nelson, 1963) Hearing sensitive could extend beyond the visual field.
- (Nieder, 2023) Because elasmobranchs lack a gas-filled swim bladder to sense sound pressure, they rely on their inner ear and lateral line to detect the vector of particle motion
- (Myrberg, 1978) A silky shark approaching a sound source will continue its hunt as long as the signal remains consistent. however, if the sound suddenly increases in level by 20 dB with an abrupt onset, the shark will abruptly withdraw rapidly on a straight-line course, signaling a decision to conserve energy or avoid a potential threat - so perhaps this is used to avoid predators too…
- In a comparison of three species of shark it was found that the more pelagic species had a higher bandwidth of frequencies they could listen to whereas benthic sharks had a lower bandwidth (Nieder, 2023) This suggests that active hunters exploit a wider range of acoustic frequencies to detect prey vibrations in the open water column.

**Lead:** Olfaction extends the search further — predators can follow chemical trails, though the signal is most reliable when paired with hydrodynamic cues.
**Development:**
- (Gardiner, 2007) While not an HMS, smooth dogfish pinpoint food by tracking odor-flavored vorticity eddies left in a prey's wake. In experiments, intact sharks ignored "oozing" odor sources to strike objects that generated major wake turbulence flavored with squid rinse, proving that the combination of odor and turbulence is a more "convincing" indicator of food than odor alone
- (Atema, 1980) It could be that Yellowfin tuna utilize a "low-threshold olfactory sense" to form chemical search images—neural activity patterns that allow them to detect and follow the odor trails of prey schools that are far beyond their visual range
- There are also different levels of development in this in different species depending on how much they rely on the sense.
- For example "The results of our study suggest a new theory for the evolution of the "hammer": enhanced olfactory tracking capabilities as a result of increased temporal resolution for klinotaxis." Basically the width of the hammer allows for better direction sensing of smells (Gardiner, 2010)

**Lead:** At closer range, vision takes over — adapted in many species for the dim conditions of depth.
**Development:**
- (Fritsches, 2005) To maintain visual resolution in the dark, cold depths of the "twilight zone," swordfish utilize a specialized heating organ to warm their retinas 10∘C to 15∘C above ambient water temperature. This warming radically improves their temporal resolution (flicker fusion frequency), allowing them to detect and pursue fast-moving, cold-blooded squid that would otherwise appear as a blur.
- (Fritsches, 2003) Blue marlin maximize their ability to hunt at depth by sacrificing visual detail for extreme sensitivity. Their retinas pool photoreceptors into ganglion cells at a 40:1 ratio, and they possess larger, more sensitive cones on the top of their eyes to detect the silhouettes of prey against the dim light coming from above
- (Gardiner, 2014) For ram-feeding species like the blacktip shark, vision is the primary tool for long-distance strikes. They orient toward prey from a distance of over 2 meters (238.9 cm) and execute a direct, rapid acceleration. If blinded, these same sharks can still strike but only from a distance of a few centimeters, relying instead on their lateral line.

**Lead:** At the final centimeters, some predators switch to electroreception to guide the precise timing and orientation of the strike.
**Development:**
- (Rangel-Morales, 2025) According to the obtained results, the oceanic species analyzed have a lower average number of pores compared to those from coastal zones, as it is hypothesized that they can take better advantage of vision to do their hunting in the pelagic.
- (Gardiner, 2014) Only when the animal is within centimeters of the target does it switch to electroreception to guide the precise orientation and timing of the strike
- (Rangel-Morales, 2025) Furthermore, electroreceptive orientation decreases as the distance from the source increases, since field strength decreases dramatically with distance

**Lead:** The pattern of sensory development across species reflects the niche each animal inhabits — pelagic environments have shaped the physiology, and therefore the behaviors, available for closing on prey.
**Development:**
- Highly developed vision and hearing, reduced electroreception, and variable olfaction all point to the same conclusion: the open-water environment selects for the senses that extend range and trades off those that only work up close.

**Lead:** Together, migration, regional search, and final-mile targeting describe the full arc of how a pelagic predator finds food.
**Development:**
- The hierarchy spans more than seven orders of magnitude: a single bluefin tuna may cross an ocean basin, locate a productive eddy, and strike an individual sardine — each requiring a different set of behaviours at a different scale.
- The levels connect sequentially: migration delivers the animal to a foraging region; regional search narrows it to a productive feature; final-mile targeting closes the distance to individual prey.
