**Type**: Synthetic Exposition

**Lead:** Prey in the open ocean does not distribute uniformly — it concentrates in a small number of structured oceanographic features, and finding food means finding these features.
**Development:**


**Lead:** Anticyclonic eddies are among the most productive foraging grounds in the open ocean.
**Development:**
Fish use mesoscale eddies — rotating bodies of water 10–100 km wide — as structured foraging grounds (Arostegui, 2022). Diverse predators, including bigeye tuna, blue marlin, and striped marlin, show a significant preference for the cores of anticyclonic eddies (ACEs), where catch rates are consistently higher than in surrounding waters, though the preference is not universal across all species (Arostegui, 2022). The structural basis of this productivity lies in the eddies' thermal properties: ACEs feature anomalously warm water at depth, which displaces isotherms downward (Arostegui, 2022; Braun, 2019). For ectothermic predators like blue sharks, this warm core acts as a "conduit" — overcoming the physiological thermal constraints that would otherwise limit access to the massive biomass of the mesopelagic twilight zone at 200–1,000 m depth (Braun, 2019). The eddy is thus not merely an incidental concentration mechanism but a physical structure that extends predators' vertical reach.

**Lead:** Likewise, temperature fronts concentrate prey and predators alike.
**Development:**
Airborne lidar surveys show that both fish schools — including anchovy and sardine — and solitary predators are located significantly closer to sea surface temperature (SST) fronts than would be expected by chance (Reese, 2011). Evidence suggests that schooling fish can detect slight changes in thermal gradients and actively select to associate with them (Reese, 2011). Predators follow suit: blue sharks intensify their foraging activity in high-chlorophyll frontal areas, reducing their diving depth and increasing vertical transit rates to remain within productive layers (Queiroz, 2017). The front thus functions as a shared attractor — drawing both prey and predator toward the same narrow band of water.

**Lead:** Seamounts offer a more persistent focal point — fixed structures to which some predators return repeatedly, long after eddies have dissipated and fronts have shifted.
**Development:**
Some yellowfin tuna exhibit surprising site fidelity, remaining within 70 km of specific seamounts such as Cardno and Bonaparte for over 100 days to forage (Wright, 2021).

**Lead:** Not all concentrating features are fixed to geography: the deep scattering layer — a dense band of mesopelagic organisms that migrates vertically with the light cycle — offers a massive, mobile prey resource that many predators track daily.
**Development:**
Rather than searching the entire water column at random, evidence suggests that visual hunters time their own vertical migrations to intersect with this resource: diving deeper during the day and returning to surface waters at night to synchronize their movements with the concentration of mesopelagic prey (Andrzejaczek, 2023). But exploiting this predictability requires first finding the layer — a problem that demands its own solution.

**Lead:** Without prior knowledge of where these features are, predators must search — and the strategies they use scale with the density of prey they encounter.
**Development:**


**Lead:** At the horizontal scale, predators switch between two search modes depending on prey density.
**Development:**
In prey-sparse, oligotrophic waters, marine predators such as silky sharks and bigeye tuna adopt Lévy flights — movement patterns characterized by clusters of small steps interspersed with occasional long-distance jumps — to locate new patches (Humphries, 2010; Humphries, 2012). When these same animals enter productive hotspots such as continental shelves or thermal fronts, they shift to Brownian motion for intensive, localized search. The key adaptation is the switching itself: reading environmental context and changing strategy accordingly (Humphries, 2010; Humphries, 2012). Energy state can modulate this further: blue sharks with high plasma triglycerides exhibit directed, "transient" behavior, ranging widely across the ocean in search of distant resource patches, while sharks with low energy reserves are often confined to convoluted, "resident" movements as they search in their immediate vicinity (Gallagher, 2026).

**Lead:** The same two-mode logic plays out in the vertical dimension, encoded in the geometry of a dive.
**Development:**
When a predator is sampling — testing whether a depth layer warrants exploitation — the trajectory is a V-shape: a rapid descent and return that covers depth efficiently (Queiroz, 2017). When a layer proves productive, the dive flattens into a sustained U, the predator lingering to forage (Queiroz, 2017). Swordfish and shortfin mako sharks, for instance, regularly descend into the twilight zone at 200–1,000 m and occasionally the midnight zone below 1,000 m, tracing these patterns as they track the deep scattering layer (Braun, 2023). Evidence suggests that some yellowfin tuna perform spike dives that may represent attempts to gain navigational cues by sampling the water column — a V-dive in form and function, but aimed at orientation rather than prey (Horton, 2025).

**Lead:** Random search is not the only tool: the same oceanographic features that concentrate prey also generate detectable signals — chemical gradients, thermal plumes, turbulence — that predators can follow to mount a directed approach.
**Development:**
Predators can exploit the physical texture of these features directly through their lateral line, detecting bulk flow and turbulence to orient rheotactically or track the flavored turbulence trails of an eddy — a process known as eddy chemotaxis (Gardiner, 2007). Productive features like ACEs also tend to support higher levels of primary and secondary productivity, generating chemical gradients or odor plumes that predators can detect at a distance (Arostegui, 2022). The sensory range that makes such detection possible is considerable: evidence suggests that salmonids can detect certain substances at concentrations as low as 10−11M — equivalent to a single 50 μl drop dissolved in a 500,000-liter pool (Kasumyan, 2004). Yellowfin tuna appear capable of similar feats, with evidence suggesting they can detect the amino constituents of prey odors at the same threshold (Atema, 1980). Together, these senses allow a predator to shift from open-water search to directed pursuit well before visual contact.
