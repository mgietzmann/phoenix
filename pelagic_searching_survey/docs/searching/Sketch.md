**Lead:** Prey in the open ocean does not distribute uniformly — it concentrates in a small number of structured oceanographic features, and finding food means finding these features.
**Development:**

**Lead:** Anticyclonic eddies are among the most productive foraging grounds in the open ocean.
**Development:**
- (Arostegui, 2022) Fish use mesoscale eddies—rotating bodies of water 10–100 km wide—as structured foraging grounds.
- (Arostegui, 2022) Diverse predators, including bigeye tuna, blue marlin, and striped marlin, show a significant preference for the cores of anticyclonic eddies (ACEs), where catch rates are consistently higher than in surrounding waters (although it is not all species)
- (Arostegui, 2022) (Braun, 2019) ACEs feature anomalously warm water at depth, which displaces isotherms downward
- (Braun, 2019) Ectothermic predators like blue sharks use these warm cores as "conduits" to overcome physiological thermal constraints, allowing them to access the massive biomass of the mesopelagic "twilight zone" (200–1,000 m)

**Lead:** Likewise temperature fronts concentrate prey and predators alike.
**Development:**
- (Reese, 2011) Airborne lidar data shows that both fish schools (e.g., anchovy and sardine) and solitary predators are located significantly closer to SST fronts than would be expected by chance.
- (Reese, 2011) Schooling fish appear to detect slight changes in thermal gradients and actively select to associate with them
- (Queiroz, 2017) Predators like blue sharks intensify their foraging activity in high-chlorophyll frontal areas, reducing their diving depth and increasing their vertical transit rates to remain within productive layers

**Lead:** Seamounts act as persistent focal points for foraging.
**Development:**
- (Wright, 2021) While some species are transoceanic, yellowfin tuna often show surprising site fidelity, remaining within 70 km of specific seamounts like Cardno and Bonaparte for over 100 days to forage.

**Lead:** The deep scattering layer — a dense band of mesopelagic organisms that migrates vertically with the light cycle — provides a massive, mobile prey resource that many predators track daily.
**Development:**
- (Andrzejaczek, 2023) Instead of searching the entire water column, these visual hunters dive deeper during the day and remain in surface waters at night to synchronize their movements with the concentration of mesopelagic prey


**Lead:** Without prior knowledge of where these features are, predators must search — and the strategies they use scale with the density of prey they encounter.
**Development:**

**Lead:** At the horizontal scale, predators switch between two search modes depending on prey density.
**Development:**
- (Humphries, 2010)(Humphries, 2012) In prey-sparse, oligotrophic waters, marine predators like silky sharks and bigeye tuna adopt Lévy flights, characterized by clusters of small steps interspersed with occasional long-distance "jumps" to find new patches. When these same animals enter productive "hotspots" like continental shelves or thermal fronts, they switch to Brownian motion, a more uniform and intensive localized search.
- (Gallagher, 2026) Blue sharks with high energy stores (plasma triglycerides) exhibit directed, "transient" behavior, ranging across the ocean to seek distant resource patches. Conversely, sharks with low energy reserves are often restricted to convoluted, "resident" movements as they search for food in their immediate vicinity

**Lead:** Vertically, predators use structured diving behaviour to sample depth layers.
**Development:**
- (Braun, 2023) Predators like swordfish and shortfin mako sharks regularly dive into the twilight zone (200–1,000 m) and occasionally the midnight zone (below 1,000 m) to track the deep scattering layer
- (Horton, 2025) Yellowfin exhibit spike dives that may represent attempts to gain navigational cues by sampling the water column.
- (Queiroz, 2017) V-dives are likely used to search the water column quickly whereas U-dives seem to indicate foraging.

**Lead:** Random search is not the only tool: predators can detect the chemical and hydrodynamic signatures of productive features at a distance, allowing for more directed approaches once within range.
**Development:**
- (Gardiner, 2007) Predators use their lateral line to detect the bulk flow and turbulence within these features. This allows for rheotaxis (orienting to the flow) and eddy chemotaxis (tracking the trail of flavored turbulence) to navigate within the feature
- (Arostegui, 2022) Productive features like ACEs often exhibit higher levels of primary and secondary productivity, which create chemical gradients or odor plumes that predators can detect at a distance to find the feature
- (Kasumyan, 2004) Salmonids can detect certain substances at concentrations as low as 10−11M; at this level, a fish could record a response to a single drop (50 μl) of gall dissolved in a 500,000-liter pool
-	(Atema, 1980) Yellowfin tuna can detect amino constituents of prey odors at 10−11M
