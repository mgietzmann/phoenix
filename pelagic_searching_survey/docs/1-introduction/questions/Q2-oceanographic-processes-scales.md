# Question: What processes drive the conditions for primary productivity in the pelagic?

## Current Question
> What specific physical and oceanographic processes — bathymetric, meteorological, geological, and hydrodynamic — suspend the nutrients (iron, phosphate, nitrate, and silicon) in the water column and keep phytoplankton above their compensation depth, enabling primary productivity in the pelagic? Specifically what are the names of these processes or features?

---

## Orient

**Audience**
Marine biologists and ecologists; readers may not have specialist knowledge of pelagic oceanography and will need the picture painted.

**Purpose**
To catalogue the physical processes that drive primary productivity — naming and distinguishing bathymetric, meteorological, and hydrodynamic mechanisms. This serves as a taxonomy question: the answer produces a named list of drivers that can then be interrogated individually for scale, predictability, and geography in follow-up questions. Together those follow-ups complete the needle-in-a-haystack argument: the conditions for prey are not just specific, they are patchy and ephemeral.

---

## Sharpen

**Specificity**
Specific enough to generate a bounded answer — the four process categories (bathymetric, meteorological, geological, hydrodynamic) and two bloom conditions (nutrient suspension, compensation depth) constrain the scope without limiting it to a single ocean, season, or species. Generalizes across the global pelagic as intended.

**Falsifiability**
A satisfying answer is a well-bounded taxonomy of named mechanisms drawn from synthesis literature. The list would be considered incomplete if a documented bloom could not be attributed to any mechanism on it. Both the positive case (synthesis) and the negative case (unexplained bloom) are findable in the literature.

**Essentiality**
Essential — you cannot ask targeted follow-up questions about scale and ephemerality without a taxonomy of mechanisms first. Additionally, naming the mechanisms may itself be sufficient: if the reader already has intuitions about coastal upwelling or mesoscale eddies, the patchiness conclusion may follow without needing to argue it explicitly. The follow-up questions on scale are held in reserve depending on how much work the taxonomy does on its own.

**Causal depth**
Two conditions need establishing: (1) that blooms require specific nutrients (iron, phosphate, nitrate, silicon) suspended in the water column, and (2) that phytoplankton must be kept above their compensation depth to photosynthesize. The question then asks what named processes deliver those two conditions. Causal depth is deliberately shallow — the reader doesn't need to understand the physics of *why* an eddy rotates, only that it is a named mechanism that produces the required conditions.

---

## Situate

**Sufficiency**
The taxonomy may be sufficient on its own — if the named mechanisms carry enough intuitive weight with the reader, patchiness and ephemerality follow implicitly. If not, targeted follow-up questions on the timescales of each named process are the natural next step.

**Related questions**
- Q1: What conditions does prey require to aggregate? (prerequisite)
- Potential follow-ups: At what timescales does [process X] operate? (one per named mechanism, as needed)

**Sequencing**
Depends on Q1. The significance of process-scale and ephemerality only lands once the reader understands what those processes are producing.
