**Lead:** Understanding how highly migratory species search has required three overlapping traditions of inquiry — sensory biology, mathematical theory, and large-scale tracking — each opening a different window onto the problem as new technologies made it possible.
**Development:**
- Sensory and behavioral work is the oldest tradition: controlled experiments and anatomical study of individual animals in tanks and reef settings date to the 1960s (Nelson, 1963) and established the foundational picture of what HMS can detect
- Mathematical frameworks for search behavior began with Viswanathan's 1999 landmark paper, which established Lévy flight theory as the starting point for studying how animals search — but for years had no direct empirical test at the scale of actual animal movements
- Observing HMS at large scales required first being able to find them: before satellite tagging, studying migration and searching depended on physically recapturing tagged individuals — itself a needle-in-a-haystack problem that severely constrained what could be learned
- Satellite tagging, combined with remotely sensed environmental data, broke that constraint: for the first time it became possible to follow individual animals continuously across ocean basins, linking behavior to environment without ever needing to see the animal again
- Each window has revealed a different layer of the problem, and each has depended on technology — from electrode and behavioral apparatus to satellite — that did not previously exist

**Lead:** The earliest and most direct window into searching behavior came from laboratory and field studies probing the sensory equipment HMS carry and how they deploy it in the final approach to prey.
**Development:**

**Lead:** Behavioral experiments — exposing animals to controlled odor pulses or prey rinses under manipulated sensory conditions — have been the primary method for characterizing olfactory responses in HMS.
**Development:**
- (Gardiner, 2010) They analyzed video film records of behavioral responses to controlled odor pulses
- (Gardiner, 2014) Data came from laboratory trials of three shark species with different senses experimentally blocked
- (Atema, 1980) Data were derived from behavioral tests of captive tuna exposed to prey rinses

**Lead:** Visual capabilities have been investigated through two complementary approaches: anatomical study of eye structure and physiological measurement of retinal function.
**Development:**
- (Fritsches, 2003) Data came from an anatomical study of blue marlin eyes
- (Fritsches, 2005) They used physiological measurements of flicker fusion frequency across a range of temperatures
- (Gardiner, 2010) They analyzed video film records of behavioral responses to controlled odor pulses
- (Gardiner, 2014) Data came from laboratory trials of three shark species with different senses experimentally blocked
- (Somiya, 2000) Data came from anatomical and chemical analysis of retinal tapeta

**Lead:** Magnetoreception has been studied through displacement experiments testing orientation responses, and through histological analysis of sensory structures — together establishing both the physical basis and behavioral expression of this sense.
**Development:**
- (Keller, 2021) Data came from magnetic displacement experiments with 20 bonnethead sharks and a meta-analysis of four genetic datasets
- (Rangel-Morales, 2025) utilized histological and morphological analysis of shark heads obtained from artisanal fisheries

**Lead:** Acoustic sensitivity has been characterized through field playback trials that test behavioral attraction to sound, and electrophysiological recording of auditory brainstem responses that establish hearing thresholds.
**Development:**
- (Nelson, 1963) performed playback trials on reefs, transmitting different sound types—low-frequency pulsed (20–60 Hz), low-frequency continuous, and high-frequency pulsed (400–600 Hz)—from an underwater transducer while observers monitored shark approaches and oriented attitudes from the surface
- (Nieder, 2023) Subdermal needle electrodes recorded brainstem responses to pure-tone bursts (80–1,200 Hz), and thresholds were derived both visually and statistically using an x-intercept method
- (Myrberg, 1978) In the "attraction" phase, sharks were brought within 10 meters of an underwater speaker using irregular pulses; when they reached this criterion distance, the researchers switched to an abrupt test sound (such as a killer whale scream or synthetic noiseband) that was 20 dB louder than the attraction signal

**Lead:** Mathematical modeling has subsequently been used to propose and test how larger-scale searching strategies might work — moving the question from what an animal can detect to how it should move through space.
**Development:**
- (De Luca, 2014) They developed a stochastic adaptive network model to analyze the effects of fishing on social traditions
- (Guttal, 2010) They used evolutionary modeling of simulated populations to weigh social tendencies against migratory goals
- (Patterson, 2018) They used a multi-decadal electronic tagging dataset analyzed with behavior-switching models
- (Viswanathan, 1999) Data came from an analysis of foraging flight lengths/times in insects, mammals, and birds

**Lead:** Satellite tagging and remote sensing of the ocean environment finally made large-scale movement directly observable — unlocking the ability to ask not just where HMS go, but how and why they search at ocean scale.
**Development:**

**Lead:** Satellite and archival tags — PSATs, SPOT tags, miniPATs — have been the primary tool for recording the movements of free-ranging HMS, providing the tracks against which searching hypotheses can be tested.
**Development:**
- (Andrzejaczek, 2023) Data were derived from 66 blue marlin tagged with Pop-up Satellite Archival Tags (PSATs) between 2001 and 2021
- (Braun, 2019) Data came from 15 blue sharks tagged with SPOT and miniPAT tags, integrated with satellite sea surface height maps
- (Braun, 2023) This study synthesized electronic tag data, global shipboard acoustics surveys, and ocean models
- (Gallagher, 2026) Data were collected by measuring plasma triglycerides and tracking 13 male sharks via SPOT tags
- (Horton, 2025) Data were provided by 45 satellite-tagged bluefin tuna analyzed via state-space models
- (Hueter, 2013) Data included photo-identification and tracking from 813 conventionally tagged and 35 satellite-tagged sharks
- (Humphries, 2010) They analyzed 12 million data points from electronic tags on 14 species of marine fish
- (Humphries, 2012) Data came from GPS tracking and stomach temperature loggers deployed on 88 birds
- (Patterson, 2018) They used a multi-decadal electronic tagging dataset analyzed with behavior-switching models
- (Queiroz, 2017) Data were provided by high-resolution archival tags retrieved from 13 sharks
- (Relano, 2022) Data were synthesized from tagging and genetic literature for 11 species and Sea Around Us catch maps
- (Vandeperre, 2014) Data were collected from 37 sharks tagged with SPOT and PSAT tags in the Azores
- (Vaudo, 2017) They used satellite telemetry data from 26 sharks tagged off the USA and Mexico
- (Wright, 2021) Data were derived from 4,049 conventional tags and dozens of archival and satellite tags

**Lead:** Satellite remote sensing and fisheries datasets provide the environmental layer needed to interpret tag tracks — linking animal positions to the oceanographic features that structure prey availability.
**Development:**
- (Arostegui, 2022) Their data consisted of 23 years of Hawaiian longline fishery logbook records co-located with satellite-derived eddy trajectories and Argo float profiles
- (Braun, 2019) Data came from 15 blue sharks tagged with SPOT and miniPAT tags, integrated with satellite sea surface height maps
- (Braun, 2023) This study synthesized electronic tag data, global shipboard acoustics surveys, and ocean models
- (Reese, 2011) Data came from airborne lidar surveys and concurrent sea surface temperature map

**Lead:** No single method yields a complete picture of how HMS search; these traditions are complementary by necessity, each revealing a scale of organization the others cannot access, and each has become possible only with the technology of its era.
**Development:**
- Sensory and behavioral work establishes what individual animals can detect — the hardware available to the searching animal — but cannot follow animals at sea
- Mathematical modeling proposes how animals should move given that hardware, but operates on theory in the absence of direct observation at scale
- Satellite tracking observes movement across ocean basins, but without sensory and theoretical context cannot interpret what drives the patterns it records
- Each tradition has required its own generation of technology: electrode and behavioral apparatus for sensory work; computational capacity for stochastic and evolutionary modeling; satellite miniaturization and remote sensing for tracking
- Together, they provide complementary answers to complementary questions — what animals can detect, how they should move, and where they actually go — and no account of HMS searching behavior is complete without all three
