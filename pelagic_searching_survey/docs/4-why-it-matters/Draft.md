**Type**: Discussion

**Lead:** The picture that has emerged from tracking HMS makes clear that effective spatial protection cannot be built around fixed boundaries — the ocean these animals depend on is always moving.
**Development:**
The features that aggregate these predators — anticyclonic eddies, SST fronts — are transient, appearing and disappearing over weeks or months (Arostegui, 2022), which means that drawing a line around a fixed patch of ocean will often fail to capture the habitat where foraging actually occurs. Work in Palau illustrates this directly: the national marine sanctuary there was not large enough to encompass the distributions of tagged blue marlin, whose home ranges were up to four times larger than the protected area (Filous, 2022). There are exceptions — semi-resident individuals with low movement rates may benefit from static closures (Boerder, 2019), and this heterogeneity in mobility is worth accounting for in management design. But dynamic management has already demonstrated that real-time, responsive approaches can work: bycatch reductions have been achieved for North Atlantic right whales, loggerhead sea turtles, and southern bluefin tuna using exactly these methods (Boerder, 2019). Effective protection requires management as dynamic as the species being protected.

**Lead:** What we've learned about socially transmitted navigation tells us that fishing pressure on the largest, oldest individuals does more than reduce population size — it can permanently erase the accumulated route knowledge on which entire migrations depend.
**Development:**
In schooling species, navigation is not distributed evenly across the group. A small proportion of experienced leaders hold the directional knowledge, while the majority follow (Guttal, 2010) — which means that knowledge is concentrated in precisely the individuals most targeted by size-selective fishing. The consequences can be irreversible. Atlantic bluefin tuna abruptly disappeared from the Norwegian Sea following the removal of experienced leaders through overfishing; the route has not recovered (De Luca, 2014). This is a fundamentally different kind of loss than the depletion of biomass. Population biomass can, in principle, regenerate given sufficient time and reduced pressure. Social memory cannot: once the individuals who hold a route are gone, the route itself is gone. Standard stock assessments have no way of capturing this, which means that even a recovering population may be permanently impoverished in its capacity to exploit historical foraging grounds.

**Lead:** Understanding how tightly HMS search strategies are tied to specific oceanographic structures reveals that climate change will not just shift where these animals go — it will disrupt the environmental cues their strategies depend on.
**Development:**
The subtropical gyres that characterize much of the open ocean — already low in productivity and sparse in prey — are predicted to expand under global warming (Arostegui, 2022), compressing the productive margins on which foraging aggregations depend. Over longer time scales, plankton abundance and species composition may shift substantially: in some regions, increased stratification and suppressed upwelling will drive declines in phytoplankton and zooplankton biomass, while in others, strengthened winds may enhance nutrient supply and increase production (Lalli, 2006). For migrants relying on traditional environmental cues to time and route their movements, these shifts introduce the possibility of multiple mismatches — temporal, if fish follow cues that are no longer linked to favorable conditions along the route, and spatial, if they arrive at foraging grounds that are no longer productive (Lennox, 2019). Compounding this, the expansion and shoaling of oxygen minimum zones in regions like the eastern Atlantic is already restricting both predators and their prey to oxygenated surface layers (Andrzejaczek, 2023), concentrating animals in ways that alter encounter rates and catchability. Even where conditions improve in isolation — higher SSTs enabling blue marlin to make deeper and longer mesopelagic dives — the net effect on search efficiency will depend on how the full suite of interacting changes resolves (Andrzejaczek, 2023), a complexity that simple projections of distribution shifts cannot capture.

**Lead:** Given what we now know about the centrality of olfaction to both navigation and prey detection, chemical pollution poses a threat beyond direct toxicity — it can degrade the sensory infrastructure on which HMS depend to find food and return to spawning grounds.
**Development:**
Exposure to heavy metals — mercury, copper, zinc — and to detergents causes structural disturbances and complete degeneration of olfactory receptor cells (Kasumyan, 2004). The fish may survive the exposure but lose the sensory capacity on which navigation and foraging depend. pH presents a parallel problem: the olfactory response of salmonids is already reduced at pH levels below 6.5, and CO2 concentrations and acidification may further impair olfactory function during spawning migrations (Bett, 2016). For species relying on chemical gradients to locate spawning grounds or track prey patches, degradation of this sensory channel is not a sublethal effect in the ordinary sense — it strikes at the mechanism of search itself.

**Lead:** Taken together, these cases illustrate the point made at the outset: clearly understanding the mechanisms of search is a requirement in order to manage these animals correctly.
**Development:**
Each of the threats reviewed here — the mismatch between static protection and dynamic habitat, the irreversible loss of socially held route knowledge, the disruption of the oceanographic cues that orient search, the chemical degradation of olfactory navigation — operates through a common pathway: it interferes with how HMS find what they need in a spatially and temporally variable ocean. Managing catch rates or protecting fixed areas, without accounting for these mechanisms, risks missing the ways in which populations can be harmed even when conventional metrics appear stable. The search strategies documented in this review are not peripheral biology — they are the functional core of what these species are, and understanding them is a precondition for managing them well.
