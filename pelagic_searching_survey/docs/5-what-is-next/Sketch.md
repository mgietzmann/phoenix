**Lead:** Significant opportunities remain open across this field: in physiological monitoring, observational infrastructure, environmental stressors, and integrative study design.
**Development:**

**Lead:** Tracking tells us where HMS go, but not what they are experiencing — closing that gap requires new tools to monitor physiological state and foraging success in real time.
**Development:**
- (Gallagher, 2026) Future tools are needed for repeated sampling of blood or metabolic indicators while an animal is being tracked to understand how energy reserves fluctuate during search cycles
- (Queiroz, 2017) (Braun, 2023) (Andrzejaczek, 2023) There is a call to utilize technologies such as oxygen sensor tags, animal-borne video, and gastric pH meters to directly quantify foraging events at depth and disentangle whether dives are driven by thermal preferences or prey availability

**Lead:** The tools we already have — satellite tags, occurrence datasets, habitat models — need to be extended in scope and sharpened in resolution to better characterize pelagic search behavior.
**Development:**
- (Filous, 2022) (Queiroz, 2019)  A high priority is developing smaller, more robust satellite tags attachable to diverse life stages and species currently too small to track
- (Queiroz, 2019) There is an urgent need for more fishery-independent occurrence data to identify predator "hotspots"
- (Braun, 2023) Future models need to move beyond simple depth predictions to incorporate variations in biomass and community composition within different scattering layers (e.g., shallow vs. deep-bathypelagic) to better reflect real-world search conditions

**Lead:** How environmental stressors — pollution, acidification — affect the sensory systems HMS rely on for navigation and prey detection requires further investigation.
**Development:**
- (Bett, 2016) A critical need exists to research the impact of toxins and ocean acidification on the olfactory systems and behavioral assays of migrating fish to define how pollution impairs their ability to find prey and spawning grounds

**Lead:** The greatest opportunity lies in synthesis: tracing a single species' full search strategy — from sensory detection through navigational decision to foraging outcome — across the range of cues and conditions it actually encounters.
**Development:**
- These studies all demonstrate what could be but very few in any actually give us a complete picture for any specific species.
- (Bett, 2016) (Lennox, 2019) Research is needed to evaluate the relative importance of imprinted, conspecific, and non-olfactory environmental cues through integrated approaches rather than odorant-specific experiments
- Very few of these studies map this all out from beginning to end.
