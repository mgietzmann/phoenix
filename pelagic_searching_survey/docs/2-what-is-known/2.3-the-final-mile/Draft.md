**Lead:** Sound travels far in water, and many predators are tuned to the signals of struggling prey.
**Development:**
Field experiments have shown that sharks do not home in on continuous tones but instead orient toward the low-frequency "thrumming" vibrations produced by a speared fish struggling against a reef (Nelson, 1963) — a distinction that may extend effective hearing ranges well beyond the visual field. The signal this sensitivity decodes is not simply a trigger to approach. A silky shark will continue tracking a consistent sound source, but if the signal abruptly jumps by 20 dB, the animal withdraws on a straight-line course — a response that may serve both energy conservation and predator avoidance (Myrberg, 1978). Across species, the available bandwidth tracks ecological niche: pelagic sharks can detect a wider range of frequencies than their benthic relatives, suggesting that active hunters in the open water column exploit a broader acoustic window for detecting prey (Nieder, 2023).

**Lead:** Olfaction extends the search further — predators can follow chemical trails well beyond the visual field.
**Development:**
Evidence suggests that some yellowfin tuna possess a low-threshold olfactory sense capable of forming chemical search images — neural activity patterns that allow them to detect and follow the odor trails of prey schools well beyond their visual range (Atema, 1980). The degree to which olfaction is developed varies across species in proportion to how heavily they depend on it; the most striking morphological example is the hammerhead shark, whose laterally extended head is proposed to enhance olfactory tracking by increasing the temporal resolution of klinotaxis — the wider the spread, the more precisely the animal can resolve the direction of a chemical gradient (Gardiner, 2010).

**Lead:** At closer range, vision becomes available — adapted in many species for the dim conditions of depth.
**Development:**
In many species it has been adapted not for acuity but for sensitivity in the dim conditions of depth. Swordfish carry a specialized heating organ that warms their retinas 10 to 15°C above the ambient temperature of the deep scattering layer; the warming sharply improves their temporal resolution, or flicker fusion frequency, enabling them to track fast-moving squid that would otherwise appear as a blur in cold water (Fritsches, 2005). Blue marlin take a different approach to the same problem, sacrificing fine visual detail in favor of extreme light sensitivity: their retinas pool photoreceptors into ganglion cells at a 40:1 ratio, and they carry larger, more sensitive cones on the dorsal surface of the eye, angled to detect the silhouettes of prey against the dim downwelling light (Fritsches, 2003). Together, these adaptations trace a common pressure: in the open water column, finding prey before it can evade is the central problem, and visual systems have been shaped to extend the window of detection.

**Lead:** At the final centimeters, some predators switch to electroreception to guide the precise timing and orientation of the strike.
**Development:**
But only then, because the physics of the system demand it: bioelectric field strength falls off sharply with distance, making electroreception effective only at close range (Rangel-Morales, 2025). Experiments confirm this boundary directly; a blacktip shark does not shift to electroreceptive guidance until it is within centimeters of its target (Gardiner, 2014). The reduced reliance on this sense in pelagic species may be a predictable consequence of their wider visual range: oceanic sharks have fewer ampullary pores on average than their coastal relatives, consistent with the hypothesis that animals hunting in well-lit open water can rely on vision to close most of the gap, reserving electroreception for the final, precise commit (Rangel-Morales, 2025).

**Lead:** The pattern of sensory development across species reflects the niche each animal inhabits — pelagic environments have shaped the physiology, and therefore the behaviors, available for closing on prey.
**Development:**
Highly developed vision and hearing, reduced electroreception, and variable olfaction all point toward the same conclusion: the open-water environment selects for senses that extend detection range and trades off those that are effective only at close quarters. The physiology, in this sense, is a record of the environment.

**Lead:** Together, migration, regional search, and final-mile targeting describe the full arc of how a pelagic predator finds food.
**Development:**
The hierarchy spans more than seven orders of magnitude: a single bluefin tuna may cross an ocean basin, locate a productive eddy, and strike an individual sardine — each transition requiring a different set of behaviors tuned to a different spatial scale. The levels connect sequentially, each stage delivering the animal to the scope of the next: migration brings the animal to a foraging region, regional search narrows it to a productive feature, and final-mile targeting closes the remaining distance to individual prey.
