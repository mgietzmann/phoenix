# Searching the Seas - Finding Prey as a Highly Migratory Species

## Introduction

The pelagic environment — the open ocean's entire water column — is the largest habitat on Earth, and like all habitats, it sustains a food web.
> Supporting
> - The pelagic is simply the open ocean from the surface waters to the greatest depths of the ocean. (Lalli, 2006)
> - The oceans occupy 71% of the Earth's surface - far more than land and freshwater combined (Lalli, 2006)
> - The ocean ranges in depth too. The deepest parts are almost 11,000m from the surface but on average oceans are ~3800m > deep (Lalli, 2006).
> - The combination means the volume of the ocean represents ~300x more habitable space than land and freshwater combined (Lalli, 2006)
> - To put this in further perspective, the continental shelves (along the coasts of the continents) underly only 8% of the total ocean, yet their combined area is about equal to Europe and South America combined (Lalli, 2006).


At the foundation of this food web sit the plankton — organisms too small or too weak to swim against the currents, and therefore entirely at the mercy of the sea.
> Supporting
> - The base of the pelagic food chain is formed by plankton - creatures who are not strong enough to swim against the currents but instead drift with them.
> - At the very base of the food chain are the phytoplankton ranging from 10's of micrometers in size to millimeters. (Lalli, 2006) These take nutrients in the water and sunlight and provide the food the rest of the food chain depends upon.
> - Feeding upon these as well as one another are the zooplankton - plankton that cannot photosynthesize their own food.
> - Copepods are an example which are usually only millimeters long (Lalli, 2006) and comprise 70% of the plankton collected in nets.
> - Yet there are also small pelagic snails whose shells measure in tens of millimeters (Lalli, 2006) and there are also salps whose individuals measure up to 30cm but can form aggregations many meters long (Lalli, 2006)
> - Jellyfish are also in this category as they too are bound to the currents of the ocean (Lalli, 2006)


As drifters, phytoplankton depend on the ocean to provide two things: nutrients and light.
> Supporting
> - To be kept at depths where the photosynthetic production outpaces metabolic losses (Lalli, 2006)
> - And for there be/have been enough mixing to bring in nutrients from the deep (Behrenfeld, 2014), or for nutrients to have come from such sources as river systems or dust falls (Behrenfeld, 2014).


A diverse array of physical and oceanographic processes can set up these conditions.
> Supporting
> - Upwelling where wind driven currents move surface waters away from the coast creating a circulation that draws colder nutrient rich water to the surface (Acha, 2015)
> - Narrow boundaries between different water masses, such as tidal fronts, upwelling fronts, and plume fronts, generate intense vertical circulations (Acha, 2015)
> - Mesoscale eddies are rotating bodies of water that have spatial scales of order 10–100 km, can persist for weeks to years trapping and transporting large water masses across vast distances and by driving the vertical flux of heat and nutrients which, in turn, can modulate primary production (Arostegui, 2022)
> - Nutrients can also be mixed into surface waters by currents interacting with intense bathymetry and geography such as islands, shelf breaks, and ragged coastlines (Acha, 2015)
> - Finally glacial melting, river discharge, and dust settling (Acha, 2015; Behrenfeld, 2014) will bring in nutrients as well.
> - While mechanical energy brings nutrients up, solar heating creates a thermocline which creates stratification that limits the depth to which phytoplankton are mixed (Acha, 2015)


These processes are highly dynamic — varying by season, geography, and timescale — making the productive environment heterogeneous and ever-changing.
> Supporting
> - What we have then is a patchy distribution of biomass suspended in the water column defined by weather, currents, and coastlines amongst other things.
> - "On the basis of global areal extent, phytoplankton blooms are uncommon" (Behrenfeld, 2014).


The nekton — krill, mesopelagic fish, and shrimp — add yet another layer of variability: they can move, and they use that motility to hide, retreating to depth during the day and surfacing only at night to feed.
> Supporting
> - These are creatures that can decide on where they end up in the water column.
> - The nekton are dominated by crustaceans like shrimp and small mesopelagic fish many species of which migrate from the depths in which they hide during the day to the surface at night to feed on plankton (Bar-On, 2019)
> - This DVM (daily vertical migration) means that prey is not at the surface waiting to be found — it is distributed throughout the water column on a cycle determined by light, not by the predator's needs.
> - This DVM includes shrimp, copepods, and krill and gives us just a sense of how massive the biomass is here with a recent estimate putting it at well over a gigatonne (1000 million tonnes) worldwide (Bar-On, 2019).


At the top of this food web sit the highly migratory species — billfish, tunas, and pelagic sharks — apex predators that range across entire ocean basins.
> Supporting
> -	(Hueter, 2013) A 7.5-meter female whale shark named "Rio Lady" sprinted 7,213 km in 150 days, crossing the equator from the Yucatan Peninsula to the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.
> -	(Andrzejaczek, 2023) Similarly, a 261 kg blue marlin maintained a displacement speed of 73.8 km per day to travel 8,853 km from Puerto Rico to the coast of Angola.
> -	(Patterson, 2018) In the Indian Ocean, a single age-2 southern bluefin tuna was tracked for over four years, covering a staggering total of 111,883 km


For these predators, finding food requires adaptability to a complex, ever-shifting environment — a suite of behavioral responses to a world that never sits still.
> Supporting
> - Light disappears quickly: in clear water, only 0.003% of surface light reaches 300 meters, limiting visibility.
> - A relatively common prey item like the pelagic shrimp may be distributed at a density of just one individual per 200–2,000 cubic meters of water.
> - And that prey is not waiting — it is either drifting with currents in patches that form and dissolve, or actively retreating from the surface into the dark.


This paper synthesizes what is known about prey-finding across all three levels — migration, searching, and the final approach — to build a coherent picture that spans ocean basin to stomach.
> Supporting 
> - Review the mechanisms at each of the three levels
> - Trace how we came to know what we know (methodological history)
> - Address why it matters for conservation and management
> - Identify what remains to be learned
> - Note the scarcity of studies that follow a single species across all three levels


## What Is Known

### Migration

At the largest scale, many pelagic predators are migratory animals — capable of crossing entire ocean basins between the foraging grounds and spawning areas that define their life cycle.
> Supporting
> - (Hueter, 2013) A 7.5-meter female whale shark named "Rio Lady" sprinted 7,213 km in 150 days, crossing the equator from the Yucatan Peninsula to the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.
> - (Andrzejaczek, 2023) Similarly, a 261 kg blue marlin maintained a displacement speed of 73.8 km per day to travel 8,853 km from Puerto Rico to the coast of Angola.
> - (Patterson, 2018) In the Indian Ocean, a single age-2 southern bluefin tuna was tracked for over four years, covering a staggering total of 111,883 km
> - (Wright, 2021) While some species are transoceanic, yellowfin tuna often show surprising site fidelity, remaining within 70 km of specific seamounts like Cardno and Bonaparte for over 100 days to forage.



Migration is an adaptation to spatiotemporal variation in the pelagic environment: because for some species the conditions that best support foraging, spawning, and seasonal survival are not co-located.
> Supporting
> - One driver is the tension between foraging and spawning requirements: the conditions that favour each are in direct conflict, necessitating migration between them.
> - (Relano, 2022) When marine predators are in a foraging phase, they target high-energy oceanographic features that aggregate prey biomass. In contrast to the nutrient-rich feeding grounds, many large pelagic species, particularly tunas, actively seek out oligotrophic (nutrient-poor) waters for spawning. This often necessitates migrations.
> - Indeed, migration has been defined as “an adaptive response to spatiotemporal variation in resources that requires indi-viduals to detect and respond to long-range and noisy environmental gradients (Shaw, 2016) For example, juvenile southern bluefin tuna undergo large-scale cyclic migrations to identify abundant food sources and maximize their rapid growth phase.
> - Then there are refuge migrations  Organisms with refuge migrations have one primary habitat (where they do most of their foraging and reproduction) that they migrate away from seasonally to seek refuge from temporarily unfavorable conditions (Shaw, 2016)
> - Migrations are therefore an adaptation that allows animals to take better advantage of the differences across the pelagic environment. 



Sustaining migrations of this scale — across thousands of kilometers of largely featureless open ocean — requires navigational machinery that is not yet fully understood.
> Supporting
> - Research has identified at least three navigational systems at work in large pelagic species: sensitivity to Earth's magnetic field, olfactory imprinting, and socially transmitted route knowledge.



Evidence from lab experiments and open-ocean tracking suggests that many pelagic species use Earth's magnetic field as a positional map, allowing them to detect where they are and navigate toward a distant destination.
> Supporting
> - (Keller, 2021) Bonnethead sharks demonstrate a "magnetic map" by orienting themselves northward when exposed to a magnetic field representing a location 600 km south of their capture site, effectively attempting to compensate for the perceived displacement.
> - (Keller, 2021) Tracking studies of wild sharks have revealed striking associations between swimming trajectory and local magnetic maxima and minima extending from seamounts to feeding grounds



A second mechanism relies on smell: animals imprint on the chemical signatures of key locations during early life, encoding a series of waypoints they can follow back to specific places years later.
> Supporting
> - (Kasumyan, 2004) Salmonids identify their home spawning rivers from dozens or hundreds of kilometers away by detecting unique odor combinations they imprinted on as juveniles during smoltification
> - (Horton, 2025) Yellowfin exhibit spike dives that may represent attempts to gain navigational cues by sampling the water column.
> - (Bett, 2016) The Sequential Imprinting Hypothesis—a modification of the Olfactory Imprinting Hypothesis—proposes that juvenile fish do not just imprint on their natal water, but also on a series of "waypoints" along their route during their initial downstream migration
> - (Bett, 2016) Field studies where juvenile salmonids are transplanted from one location and released in another show that adults frequently return to the site of release rather than their original rearing site



A positional map, however, only tells an animal where it is — not where to go; converting a map into a route requires a separate layer of directional knowledge, and how animals acquire this remains an open question.
> Supporting
> - Having a precise sense of position is not the same as knowing a destination: an animal could be magnetically and chemically literate and still have no information about which direction to move.
> - Directional knowledge could be encoded genetically, built through experience by following environmental gradients until destinations become familiar, or acquired from conspecifics who already know the route.



For schooling species, navigational knowledge may not be held by individuals but transmitted socially — dependent on experienced leaders whose loss can cause entire migration routes to collapse.
> Supporting
> - (De Luca, 2014) In schooling species, navigation is a socially facilitated process guided by collective memory. The loss of these older, knowledgeable individuals through overfishing can cause the sudden collapse of traditional migratory routes, as seen when Atlantic bluefin tuna abruptly disappeared from the Norwegian Sea after their leaders and prey were exploited.
> - (Guttal, 2010) Groups often consist of a small proportion of "informed" leaders who acquire directional information from the environment while the majority of "social" individuals follow the group.
> - (Bett, 2016) Some studies suggest that if imprinted waypoints are absent (for example, if the fish were transported past them as juveniles), the adults will instead rely on conspecific cues (pheromones) from other fish to find a suitable spawning ground.



Successful migration delivers a predator to the right part of the ocean; what happens next — locating prey within that region — is a different problem entirely.
> Supporting




### Searching

Prey in the open ocean does not distribute uniformly — it concentrates in a small number of structured oceanographic features, and finding food means finding these features.
> Supporting



Anticyclonic eddies are among the most productive foraging grounds in the open ocean.
> Supporting
> - (Arostegui, 2022) Fish use mesoscale eddies—rotating bodies of water 10–100 km wide—as structured foraging grounds.
> - (Arostegui, 2022) Diverse predators, including bigeye tuna, blue marlin, and striped marlin, show a significant preference for the cores of anticyclonic eddies (ACEs), where catch rates are consistently higher than in surrounding waters (although it is not all species)
> - (Arostegui, 2022) (Braun, 2019) ACEs feature anomalously warm water at depth, which displaces isotherms downward
> - (Braun, 2019) Ectothermic predators like blue sharks use these warm cores as "conduits" to overcome physiological thermal constraints, allowing them to access the massive biomass of the mesopelagic "twilight zone" (200–1,000 m)



Likewise temperature fronts concentrate prey and predators alike.
> Supporting
> - (Reese, 2011) Airborne lidar data shows that both fish schools (e.g., anchovy and sardine) and solitary predators are located significantly closer to SST fronts than would be expected by chance.
> - (Reese, 2011) Schooling fish appear to detect slight changes in thermal gradients and actively select to associate with them
> - (Queiroz, 2017) Predators like blue sharks intensify their foraging activity in high-chlorophyll frontal areas, reducing their diving depth and increasing their vertical transit rates to remain within productive layers



Seamounts act as persistent focal points for foraging.
> Supporting
> - (Wright, 2021) While some species are transoceanic, yellowfin tuna often show surprising site fidelity, remaining within 70 km of specific seamounts like Cardno and Bonaparte for over 100 days to forage.



The deep scattering layer — a dense band of mesopelagic organisms that migrates vertically with the light cycle — provides a massive, mobile prey resource that many predators track daily.
> Supporting
> - (Andrzejaczek, 2023) Instead of searching the entire water column, these visual hunters dive deeper during the day and remain in surface waters at night to synchronize their movements with the concentration of mesopelagic prey




Without prior knowledge of where these features are, predators must search — and the strategies they use scale with the density of prey they encounter.
> Supporting



At the horizontal scale, predators switch between two search modes depending on prey density.
> Supporting
> - (Humphries, 2010)(Humphries, 2012) When in prey-sparse, oligotrophic waters, marine predators like silky sharks and bigeye tuna adopt Lévy flights — clusters of small steps interspersed with occasional long-distance jumps to find new patches; when these same animals enter productive hotspots like continental shelves or thermal fronts, they switch to Brownian motion for intensive localized search. The key adaptation is the switching itself: reading environmental context and changing strategy accordingly.
> - (Gallagher, 2026) Blue sharks with high energy stores (plasma triglycerides) exhibit directed, "transient" behavior, ranging across the ocean to seek distant resource patches. Conversely, sharks with low energy reserves are often restricted to convoluted, "resident" movements as they search for food in their immediate vicinity



Vertically, predators use structured diving behaviour to sample depth layers.
> Supporting
> - (Braun, 2023) Predators like swordfish and shortfin mako sharks regularly dive into the twilight zone (200–1,000 m) and occasionally the midnight zone (below 1,000 m) to track the deep scattering layer
> - (Horton, 2025) Yellowfin exhibit spike dives that may represent attempts to gain navigational cues by sampling the water column.
> - (Queiroz, 2017) V-dives are likely used to search the water column quickly whereas U-dives seem to indicate foraging.



Random search is not the only tool: the same oceanographic features that concentrate prey also generate detectable signals — chemical gradients, thermal plumes, turbulence — that predators can follow to mount a directed approach.
> Supporting
> - (Gardiner, 2007) Predators use their lateral line to detect the bulk flow and turbulence within these features. This allows for rheotaxis (orienting to the flow) and eddy chemotaxis (tracking the trail of flavored turbulence) to navigate within the feature
> - (Arostegui, 2022) Productive features like ACEs often exhibit higher levels of primary and secondary productivity, which create chemical gradients or odor plumes that predators can detect at a distance to find the feature
> - (Kasumyan, 2004) Salmonids can detect certain substances at concentrations as low as 10−11M; at this level, a fish could record a response to a single drop (50 μl) of gall dissolved in a 500,000-liter pool
> -	(Atema, 1980) Yellowfin tuna can detect amino constituents of prey odors at 10−11M

Once a predator has located a productive feature, the problem changes again: from finding the patch to finding individual prey within it.
> Supporting




### The Final Mile

Having located a productive feature, predators switch from broad search to precise, multi-sensory targeting — deploying senses in sequence as they close the distance to individual prey.
> Supporting



Sound travels far in water, and many predators are tuned to the signals of struggling prey.
> Supporting
> - (Nelson, 1963) In field experiments, these predators ignore continuous tones, instead homing in on sounds that mimic the "thrumming" vibrations of a speared black grouper struggling against a reef
> - (Nelson, 1963) Hearing sensitive could extend beyond the visual field.
> - (Myrberg, 1978) A silky shark approaching a sound source will continue its hunt as long as the signal remains consistent. however, if the sound suddenly increases in level by 20 dB with an abrupt onset, the shark will abruptly withdraw rapidly on a straight-line course, signaling a decision to conserve energy or avoid a potential threat - so perhaps this is used to avoid predators too…
> - In a comparison of three species of shark it was found that the more pelagic species had a higher bandwidth of frequencies they could listen to whereas benthic sharks had a lower bandwidth (Nieder, 2023) This suggests that active hunters exploit a wider range of acoustic frequencies to detect prey vibrations in the open water column.



Olfaction extends the search further — predators can follow chemical trails well beyond the visual field.
> Supporting
> - (Atema, 1980) It could be that Yellowfin tuna utilize a "low-threshold olfactory sense" to form chemical search images—neural activity patterns that allow them to detect and follow the odor trails of prey schools that are far beyond their visual range
> - There are also different levels of development in this in different species depending on how much they rely on the sense.
> - For example "The results of our study suggest a new theory for the evolution of the "hammer": enhanced olfactory tracking capabilities as a result of increased temporal resolution for klinotaxis." Basically the width of the hammer allows for better direction sensing of smells (Gardiner, 2010)



At closer range, vision becomes available — adapted in many species for the dim conditions of depth.
> Supporting
> - (Fritsches, 2005) To maintain visual resolution in the dark, cold depths of the "twilight zone," swordfish utilize a specialized heating organ to warm their retinas 10∘C to 15∘C above ambient water temperature. This warming radically improves their temporal resolution (flicker fusion frequency), allowing them to detect and pursue fast-moving, cold-blooded squid that would otherwise appear as a blur.
> - (Fritsches, 2003) Blue marlin maximize their ability to hunt at depth by sacrificing visual detail for extreme sensitivity. Their retinas pool photoreceptors into ganglion cells at a 40:1 ratio, and they possess larger, more sensitive cones on the top of their eyes to detect the silhouettes of prey against the dim light coming from above



At the final centimeters, some predators switch to electroreception to guide the precise timing and orientation of the strike.
> Supporting
> - (Rangel-Morales, 2025) According to the obtained results, the oceanic species analyzed have a lower average number of pores compared to those from coastal zones, as it is hypothesized that they can take better advantage of vision to do their hunting in the pelagic.
> - (Gardiner, 2014) Only when the animal is within centimeters of the target does it switch to electroreception to guide the precise orientation and timing of the strike
> - (Rangel-Morales, 2025) Furthermore, electroreceptive orientation decreases as the distance from the source increases, since field strength decreases dramatically with distance



The pattern of sensory development across species reflects the niche each animal inhabits — pelagic environments have shaped the physiology, and therefore the behaviors, available for closing on prey.
> Supporting
> - Highly developed vision and hearing, reduced electroreception, and variable olfaction all point to the same conclusion: the open-water environment selects for the senses that extend range and trades off those that only work up close.



Together, migration, regional search, and final-mile targeting describe the full arc of how a pelagic predator finds food.
> Supporting
> - The hierarchy spans more than seven orders of magnitude: a single bluefin tuna may cross an ocean basin, locate a productive eddy, and strike an individual sardine — each requiring a different set of behaviours at a different scale.
> - The levels connect sequentially: migration delivers the animal to a foraging region; regional search narrows it to a productive feature; final-mile targeting closes the distance to individual prey.



How do you study animals that range across thousands of kilometers of open ocean, dive beyond the reach of divers, and hunt at speeds too fast for the human eye to follow?

## How We Know It

Understanding how highly migratory species search has required three overlapping traditions of inquiry — sensory biology, mathematical theory, and large-scale tracking — each opening a different window onto the problem as new technologies made it possible.
> Supporting
> - Sensory and behavioral work is the oldest tradition: controlled experiments and anatomical study of individual animals in tanks and reef settings date to the 1960s (Nelson, 1963) and established the foundational picture of what HMS can detect
> - Mathematical frameworks for search behavior began with Viswanathan's 1999 landmark paper, which established Lévy flight theory as the starting point for studying how animals search — but for years had no direct empirical test at the scale of actual animal movements
> - Observing HMS at large scales required first being able to find them: before satellite tagging, studying migration and searching depended on physically recapturing tagged individuals — itself a needle-in-a-haystack problem that severely constrained what could be learned
> - Satellite tagging, combined with remotely sensed environmental data, broke that constraint: for the first time it became possible to follow individual animals continuously across ocean basins, linking behavior to environment without ever needing to see the animal again
> - Each window has revealed a different layer of the problem, and each has depended on technology — from electrode and behavioral apparatus to satellite — that did not previously exist



The earliest and most direct window into searching behavior came from laboratory and field studies probing the sensory equipment HMS carry and how they deploy it in the final approach to prey.
> Supporting



Behavioral experiments — exposing animals to controlled odor pulses or prey rinses under manipulated sensory conditions — have been the primary method for characterizing olfactory responses in HMS.
> Supporting
> - (Gardiner, 2010) They analyzed video film records of behavioral responses to controlled odor pulses
> - (Gardiner, 2014) Data came from laboratory trials of three shark species with different senses experimentally blocked
> - (Atema, 1980) Data were derived from behavioral tests of captive tuna exposed to prey rinses



Visual capabilities have been investigated through two complementary approaches: anatomical study of eye structure and physiological measurement of retinal function.
> Supporting
> - (Fritsches, 2003) Data came from an anatomical study of blue marlin eyes
> - (Fritsches, 2005) They used physiological measurements of flicker fusion frequency across a range of temperatures
> - (Gardiner, 2010) They analyzed video film records of behavioral responses to controlled odor pulses
> - (Gardiner, 2014) Data came from laboratory trials of three shark species with different senses experimentally blocked
> - (Somiya, 2000) Data came from anatomical and chemical analysis of retinal tapeta



Magnetoreception has been studied through displacement experiments testing orientation responses, and through histological analysis of sensory structures — together establishing both the physical basis and behavioral expression of this sense.
> Supporting
> - (Keller, 2021) Data came from magnetic displacement experiments with 20 bonnethead sharks and a meta-analysis of four genetic datasets
> - (Rangel-Morales, 2025) utilized histological and morphological analysis of shark heads obtained from artisanal fisheries



Acoustic sensitivity has been characterized through field playback trials that test behavioral attraction to sound, and electrophysiological recording of auditory brainstem responses that establish hearing thresholds.
> Supporting
> - (Nelson, 1963) performed playback trials on reefs, transmitting different sound types—low-frequency pulsed (20–60 Hz), low-frequency continuous, and high-frequency pulsed (400–600 Hz)—from an underwater transducer while observers monitored shark approaches and oriented attitudes from the surface
> - (Nieder, 2023) Subdermal needle electrodes recorded brainstem responses to pure-tone bursts (80–1,200 Hz), and thresholds were derived both visually and statistically using an x-intercept method
> - (Myrberg, 1978) In the "attraction" phase, sharks were brought within 10 meters of an underwater speaker using irregular pulses; when they reached this criterion distance, the researchers switched to an abrupt test sound (such as a killer whale scream or synthetic noiseband) that was 20 dB louder than the attraction signal



Mathematical modeling has subsequently been used to propose and test how larger-scale searching strategies might work — moving the question from what an animal can detect to how it should move through space.
> Supporting
> - (De Luca, 2014) They developed a stochastic adaptive network model to analyze the effects of fishing on social traditions
> - (Guttal, 2010) They used evolutionary modeling of simulated populations to weigh social tendencies against migratory goals
> - (Patterson, 2018) They used a multi-decadal electronic tagging dataset analyzed with behavior-switching models
> - (Viswanathan, 1999) Data came from an analysis of foraging flight lengths/times in insects, mammals, and birds



Satellite tagging and remote sensing of the ocean environment finally made large-scale movement directly observable — unlocking the ability to ask not just where HMS go, but how and why they search at ocean scale.
> Supporting



Satellite and archival tags — PSATs, SPOT tags, miniPATs — have been the primary tool for recording the movements of free-ranging HMS, providing the tracks against which searching hypotheses can be tested.
> Supporting
> - (Andrzejaczek, 2023) Data were derived from 66 blue marlin tagged with Pop-up Satellite Archival Tags (PSATs) between 2001 and 2021
> - (Braun, 2019) Data came from 15 blue sharks tagged with SPOT and miniPAT tags, integrated with satellite sea surface height maps
> - (Braun, 2023) This study synthesized electronic tag data, global shipboard acoustics surveys, and ocean models
> - (Gallagher, 2026) Data were collected by measuring plasma triglycerides and tracking 13 male sharks via SPOT tags
> - (Horton, 2025) Data were provided by 45 satellite-tagged bluefin tuna analyzed via state-space models
> - (Hueter, 2013) Data included photo-identification and tracking from 813 conventionally tagged and 35 satellite-tagged sharks
> - (Humphries, 2010) They analyzed 12 million data points from electronic tags on 14 species of marine fish
> - (Humphries, 2012) Data came from GPS tracking and stomach temperature loggers deployed on 88 birds
> - (Patterson, 2018) They used a multi-decadal electronic tagging dataset analyzed with behavior-switching models
> - (Queiroz, 2017) Data were provided by high-resolution archival tags retrieved from 13 sharks
> - (Relano, 2022) Data were synthesized from tagging and genetic literature for 11 species and Sea Around Us catch maps
> - (Vandeperre, 2014) Data were collected from 37 sharks tagged with SPOT and PSAT tags in the Azores
> - (Vaudo, 2017) They used satellite telemetry data from 26 sharks tagged off the USA and Mexico
> - (Wright, 2021) Data were derived from 4,049 conventional tags and dozens of archival and satellite tags



Satellite remote sensing and fisheries datasets provide the environmental layer needed to interpret tag tracks — linking animal positions to the oceanographic features that structure prey availability.
> Supporting
> - (Arostegui, 2022) Their data consisted of 23 years of Hawaiian longline fishery logbook records co-located with satellite-derived eddy trajectories and Argo float profiles
> - (Braun, 2019) Data came from 15 blue sharks tagged with SPOT and miniPAT tags, integrated with satellite sea surface height maps
> - (Braun, 2023) This study synthesized electronic tag data, global shipboard acoustics surveys, and ocean models
> - (Reese, 2011) Data came from airborne lidar surveys and concurrent sea surface temperature map



No single method yields a complete picture of how HMS search; these traditions are complementary by necessity, each revealing a scale of organization the others cannot access, and each has become possible only with the technology of its era.
> Supporting
> - Sensory and behavioral work establishes what individual animals can detect — the hardware available to the searching animal — but cannot follow animals at sea
> - Mathematical modeling proposes how animals should move given that hardware, but operates on theory in the absence of direct observation at scale
> - Satellite tracking observes movement across ocean basins, but without sensory and theoretical context cannot interpret what drives the patterns it records
> - Each tradition has required its own generation of technology: electrode and behavioral apparatus for sensory work; computational capacity for stochastic and evolutionary modeling; satellite miniaturization and remote sensing for tracking
> - Together, they provide complementary answers to complementary questions — what animals can detect, how they should move, and where they actually go — and no account of HMS searching behavior is complete without all three




## Why It Matters

The picture that has emerged from tracking HMS makes clear that effective spatial protection cannot be built around fixed boundaries — the ocean these animals depend on is always moving.
> Supporting
> - (Filous, 2022) In Palau, researchers found that the national marine sanctuary was not large enough to encompass the distributions of tagged blue marlin, whose home ranges were up to four times larger than the protected area
> - (Arostegui, 2022) Predators congregate at oceanographic features like anticyclonic eddies and SST fronts to forage, but these features are transient, appearing and disappearing over weeks or months
> - (Boerder, 2019) Compared to individuals exhibiting higher mobility, ‘lazy’ semi-resident fish with low movement rates may be favored by spatial protection
> - (Boerder, 2019) Dynamic management has already proven effective in reducing bycatch for threatened species, including North Atlantic right whales, loggerhead sea turtles, and southern bluefin tuna



What we've learned about socially transmitted navigation tells us that fishing pressure on the largest, oldest individuals does more than reduce population size — it can permanently erase the accumulated route knowledge on which entire migrations depend.
> Supporting
> - (De Luca, 2014) In schooling species, navigation is guided by experienced leaders holding collective memory; the loss of these individuals through overfishing caused Atlantic bluefin tuna to abruptly disappear from the Norwegian Sea, a collapse from which the route has not recovered
> - (Guttal, 2010) Groups typically consist of a small proportion of "informed" leaders who acquire directional information from the environment, while the majority follow — meaning knowledge is concentrated in exactly the individuals most targeted by size-selective fishing
> - Unlike population biomass, social memory cannot regenerate: once the leaders who hold a route are gone, the route itself is gone



Understanding how tightly HMS search strategies are tied to specific oceanographic structures reveals that climate change will not just shift where these animals go — it will disrupt the environmental cues their strategies depend on.
> Supporting
> - (Arostegui, 2022) Subtropical gyres, which are characterized by low productivity and sparse prey, are predicted to expand in area in response to global warming
> - (Lalli, 2006) Long-term records indicate that plankton abundance and species composition may change substantially over decadal time scales. Decreasing plankton biomass may be caused by climate changes that increase water stratification and depress upwelling; conversely, in other regions, increasing winds may enhance nutrient concentrations in the euphotic zone and lead to increased phytoplankton and zooplankton production
> - (Lennox, 2019) For example, multiple mismatches could develop in relation to food availability and migration, including temporal mismatch from fish using traditional drivers of migration to pursue movements, but where the drivers are no longer linked to favorable conditions along the migration route, and spatial mismatch if fish move to foraging grounds that are no longer productive
> - (Andrzejaczek, 2023) In regions like the eastern Atlantic, the expansion and shoaling of OMZs restrict both predators and their prey to higher-oxygenated surface waters
> - (Andrzejaczek, 2023) For example, higher SSTs can increase the probability of deeper maximum dives in blue marlin, potentially enabling them to search deeper into the mesopelagic for longer periods before needing to return to the surface to re-warm (it’s gonna get complicated…)



Given what we now know about the centrality of olfaction to both navigation and prey detection, chemical pollution poses a threat beyond direct toxicity — it can degrade the sensory infrastructure on which HMS depend to find food and return to spawning grounds.
> Supporting
> - (Kasumyan, 2004) Exposure to heavy metals (such as mercury, copper, and zinc) and detergents causes deep structural disturbances and the complete degeneration of olfactory receptor cells
> - (Bett, 2016) In addition to temperature and stress, CO2 concentrations and pH levels may affect olfaction during the spawning migration. The olfactory response of salmonids is reduced at pH levels below 6.5



Taken together, these cases illustrate the point made at the outset: clearly understanding the mechanisms of search is a requirement in order to manage these animals correctly.

## What Is Next

Significant opportunities remain open across this field: in physiological monitoring, observational infrastructure, environmental stressors, and integrative study design.
> Supporting



Tracking tells us where HMS go, but not what they are experiencing — closing that gap requires new tools to monitor physiological state and foraging success in real time.
> Supporting
> - (Gallagher, 2026) Future tools are needed for repeated sampling of blood or metabolic indicators while an animal is being tracked to understand how energy reserves fluctuate during search cycles
> - (Queiroz, 2017) (Braun, 2023) (Andrzejaczek, 2023) There is a call to utilize technologies such as oxygen sensor tags, animal-borne video, and gastric pH meters to directly quantify foraging events at depth and disentangle whether dives are driven by thermal preferences or prey availability



The tools we already have — satellite tags, occurrence datasets, habitat models — need to be extended in scope and sharpened in resolution to better characterize pelagic search behavior.
> Supporting
> - (Filous, 2022) (Queiroz, 2019)  A high priority is developing smaller, more robust satellite tags attachable to diverse life stages and species currently too small to track
> - (Queiroz, 2019) There is an urgent need for more fishery-independent occurrence data to identify predator "hotspots"
> - (Braun, 2023) Future models need to move beyond simple depth predictions to incorporate variations in biomass and community composition within different scattering layers (e.g., shallow vs. deep-bathypelagic) to better reflect real-world search conditions



How environmental stressors — pollution, acidification — affect the sensory systems HMS rely on for navigation and prey detection requires further investigation.
> Supporting
> - (Bett, 2016) A critical need exists to research the impact of toxins and ocean acidification on the olfactory systems and behavioral assays of migrating fish to define how pollution impairs their ability to find prey and spawning grounds



The greatest opportunity lies in synthesis: tracing a single species' full search strategy — from sensory detection through navigational decision to foraging outcome — across the range of cues and conditions it actually encounters.
> Supporting
> - These studies all demonstrate what could be but very few in any actually give us a complete picture for any specific species.
> - (Bett, 2016) (Lennox, 2019) Research is needed to evaluate the relative importance of imprinted, conspecific, and non-olfactory environmental cues through integrated approaches rather than odorant-specific experiments
> - Very few of these studies map this all out from beginning to end.
