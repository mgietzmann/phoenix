# Prey Finding in the Open Ocean: A Three-Level Framework

The pelagic environment — the open ocean's entire water column — is the largest habitat on Earth, accounting for 99% of the planet's biosphere and 80% of the fish we harvest. It is not, however, a hospitable place to find a meal. Prey is never uniformly distributed; it is patchy and ephemeral, shifting with what might best be called the internal weather of the sea. A relatively common prey item like the pelagic shrimp may be distributed at a density of just one individual per 200–2,000 cubic meters of water. The space itself is staggering — 300 times more inhabitable volume than land and freshwater combined, structured not by terrain or vegetation but by invisible gradients of temperature, salinity, and chemistry, reorganized constantly by currents and spinning masses of water. And the light disappears fast: in clear water, only 0.003% of surface light reaches 300 meters.

Into this environment we place the highly mobile pelagic predators — tunas, billfishes, and oceanic sharks — animals capable of crossing entire ocean basins in months. A female whale shark sprinted 7,213 km in 150 days from the Yucatan Peninsula to the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. A 261 kg blue marlin traveled 8,853 km from Puerto Rico to the coast of Angola. A single southern bluefin tuna, tracked for four years, covered 111,883 km. These are not animals stumbling through a featureless void. They have somewhere to be. And when they arrive, they still have to find food.

## Three Levels of the Problem

The question of how these animals find prey can be broken into three successively more focused levels, each with its own mechanisms and its own literature.

The first is **migration**: getting from wherever you are to a productive foraging ground. Many of these animals make regular, repeated journeys to "known" locations — seamounts, upwelling zones, productive gyres — using a combination of magnetic maps, olfactory waypoints imprinted early in life, and socially transmitted knowledge passed through schools and populations. This last mechanism turns out to be surprisingly fragile: fish out the experienced individuals and the group may simply forget where to go.

The second level is **searching the foraging ground**: you've arrived in a productive region, but the region itself is not uniform. Productive patches are structured around oceanographic features — mesoscale eddies, thermal fronts, seamounts, the deep scattering layer — each of which aggregates prey in different ways and for different reasons. Animals appear to find these features through a combination of optimized random search (Lévy flights in barren waters, switching to Brownian motion near productive patches) and directed sensing, using olfaction and the lateral line to detect chemical and physical signatures of a worthwhile destination from a distance.

The third level is the **"final mile"**: the prey is near, but how do you close the gap? Here the full sensory arsenal comes into play — vision with remarkable adaptations for hunting in near-darkness, olfaction sensitive enough to detect a single drop of substance dissolved in a half-million liters, the lateral line for tracking odor-flavored turbulence left in a prey's wake, electroreception for the final centimeters, and hearing tuned to the low-frequency thrumming of struggling animals.

---

## The Goal

This paper reviews what we know across all three levels, how we came to know it, why it matters for conservation and management, and what remains to be learned. The literature is rich but scattered — across species, sensory systems, and methodological traditions that rarely speak to one another. A central aim is to pull that material into a coherent picture. And to note, honestly, how few studies have managed to follow any single species all the way from ocean basin to stomach.
