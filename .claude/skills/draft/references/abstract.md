# Guidelines: Abstract

An abstract is a self-contained summary of the whole paper. It tells the reader what the paper covers, what it argues, and why it matters — in roughly 150–300 words. The abstract is written last, from the finished draft, not from the sketch.

---

## Structure

A scientific abstract typically moves through four beats, each handled in one to three sentences:

1. **Context** — what world does this paper enter? Orient the reader to the subject and its significance without over-explaining.
2. **Problem or gap** — what challenge, question, or gap motivated the paper?
3. **Approach or content** — what does the paper actually do? For a survey, this is the scope and organizing logic; for an empirical paper, it is the methods.
4. **Findings or takeaway** — what does the reader learn? State the key result or synthesis plainly.

These beats need not be four sentences. Context and problem often merge. What matters is that all four are present and in order.

---

## Prose style

- Every sentence must pull weight. Abstracts have no room for scene-setting that doesn't advance the summary.
- Write in plain, declarative prose. No hedging, no throat-clearing.
- Match the register of the paper. If the paper is a scientific review, the abstract should sound like a scientific review.
- Do not cite sources in the abstract unless absolutely unavoidable.
- Avoid jargon that is not defined in the abstract itself — a reader who hasn't read the paper must be able to follow it.

---

## What to watch for

- **Over-summarizing the introduction**: the abstract is not a summary of the background section — it is a summary of the whole paper, including what the paper contributes.
- **Vague takeaways**: "the paper reviews the literature on X" is not a finding. What does reading the literature reveal?
- **Length creep**: if the abstract runs past 300 words, something is being explained rather than stated. Compress.
- **Mismatched emphasis**: the abstract should weight things roughly as the paper does. If the paper is mostly about search behavior and sensation, the abstract should not spend half its words on phytoplankton.
- **Listing specifics in the context paragraph**: naming individual mechanisms or examples in the opening makes it verbose. The general claim carries more than the inventory — compress to the category, not the contents.
- **Forward-reference sentences**: sentences like "how they do so is the subject of this review" are redundant when the next sentence states the scope. Cut them.
- **Over-specifying in the findings paragraph**: listing specific mechanisms in the takeaway paragraph is too granular. Name categories or themes at the right level of abstraction.
- **"From X to Y" for range**: when gesturing at a spread of implications or threats, "from X to Y" is more fluid than a colon-list.
- **Fold transitions into clauses**: rather than splitting two related ideas with a semicolon and restarting, integrate the transition into the clause itself (e.g. "assembled from studies of different species at different scales leaving synthesis an open opportunity" rather than "assembled from studies of different species; synthesis remains an open opportunity").
