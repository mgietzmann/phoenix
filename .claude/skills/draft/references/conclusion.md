# Conclusion Guidelines

## The lead must do double duty

A conclusion opens by signalling that the stated aim has been met and delivering the key finding in the same breath. A lead that merely restates the aim without landing the finding is throat-clearing.

## Compress the arc, don't enumerate it

The hierarchy, the methodological picture, and the synthesis gap should build as a single argument, not read as three items. Each clause should set up the next rather than standing alone.

## Don't re-summarize sections

The conclusion earns nothing by listing what each section said. It should state what the whole adds up to — the finding that emerges from reading everything together.

## Close with the opening forward

The final sentence is a landing, not a trailing summary. For a survey, it should point toward what comes next with confidence, not trail off on a limitation.

## Introduce no new claims

Everything in the conclusion must already be earned by the body. No new evidence, no new qualifications, no new arguments.

## Be concise

A conclusion should be short. Every sentence must earn its place. If a point is already carried by another sentence, cut it.

## Split a long opening rather than compress it

If the lead sentence tries to do too much — signalling completion of the aim and delivering the key finding — split it at a natural break rather than forcing both into one clause. Two short sentences land harder than one long one.
