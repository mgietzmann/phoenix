# Guidelines: Synthetic Exposition

Synthetic exposition summarizes and synthesizes the research of others. The author's contribution is the selection, framing, and argumentative ordering of evidence — not original data or interpretation. Tone should be confident synthesis, not hedged discussion.

---

## 1. Hedge claims to match the evidence on hand

Don't assert that animals *do* something when the evidence only *suggests* they do. Use "evidence suggests," "proposed mechanism," "may," and "can" where certainty isn't warranted. This applies especially to leads — a lead that overstates will require softening throughout the paragraph.

**Avoid:** "Animals imprint on the chemical signatures of key locations during early life."
**Prefer:** "Evidence suggests that some animals imprint on the chemical signatures of key locations during early life."

---

## 2. Structure paragraphs as a round

Each paragraph should move through three phases: topic sentence → evidence → synthesis that ties back to the topic. The closing synthesis should feel earned by the evidence, not merely restate the lead. A paragraph that ends without closing the loop leaves the reader without a takeaway.

---

## 3. Don't enumerate reasons as a list

When a paragraph covers multiple drivers or mechanisms, connect them with transitions rather than numbering them. Lists flatten prose; transitions build argument.

**Avoid:** "One driver is X. A second driver is Y. A third driver is Z."
**Prefer:** "X creates one pressure. Y creates its own. Seasonal conditions can also force movement..."

---

## 4. Keep example sentences scoped to their subject

An example about "some yellowfin tuna" should not imply all yellowfin tuna. An example drawn from one study should not be stated as a general species-level fact. Use "some," "in one example," or name the specific study subject.

**Avoid:** "Yellowfin tuna remain within 70 km of specific seamounts..."
**Prefer:** "Some yellowfin tuna remain within 70 km of specific seamounts..."

---

## 5. State the mechanism before examples that don't carry it themselves

Examples can open a paragraph, but only if they are explicit enough to carry the conceptual point on their own. If the examples don't themselves name the mechanism — leaving the reader to wait for a closing sentence to understand what they illustrated — state the mechanism first, then bring in the examples.

**Avoid:** A sequence of diving examples, then a closing sentence that finally names V vs U — forcing the reader to retroactively reinterpret everything above.
**Prefer:** Name the mechanism (V-dives for sampling, U-dives for foraging), then use examples that already make sense in that frame.

---

## 6. Avoid list-like flow between paragraphs

List-like exposition comes from paragraphs that announce their own topic without connecting to what preceded them. Leads, closing sentences, and explicit transition sentences are all available tools to build flow — use whichever fits the prose. Transition sentences may be added freely as long as they don't introduce facts or claims not in the sketch; logical bridges and framing relationships between ideas are fair game.

**Avoid:** Each paragraph opening cold with its own topic sentence, producing a sequence that reads like an enumeration.
**Prefer:** Leads that encode a relationship to the preceding paragraph ("not all concentrating features are fixed to geography..."), closing sentences that pivot forward ("but exploiting this predictability requires first finding the layer"), or brief transitional sentences between paragraphs.

---

## 7. Use past tense for historical research description

In a methods review, verbs describing what researchers *did* should be past tense. Present tense is reserved for ongoing states of the study subjects (e.g., "HMS carry olfactory organs") or for general scientific facts that remain true. Mixing tenses within a paragraph signals an oversight.

**Avoid:** "That tradition is built on controlled sensory experiments that together establish a baseline account."
**Prefer:** "That tradition was built on controlled sensory experiments that together established a baseline account."

---

## 8. Frame illustrative lists as illustrative

When a paragraph covers many examples drawn from a larger literature, signal explicitly that the list is selective. A paragraph that enumerates studies without framing implies it is trying to be comprehensive — which misrepresents the review and overstates the coverage. A single framing sentence at the open or close corrects this.

**Avoid:** A paragraph that lists every study in sequence with no indication that the literature extends beyond what is cited.
**Prefer:** Open with "The studies reviewed here illustrate the range of what this approach has made possible" or close with "These examples are illustrative rather than exhaustive — the literature extends well beyond this review."
