# Discussion Guidelines

## Order for flow, not sketch order

The sketch sequence is a checklist, not a paragraph structure. Lead with the conceptual claim that orients the reader, then bring in the evidence that backs it up. A specific example should follow the general principle it illustrates, not precede it.

## Avoid absolutes

Words like "always", "never", "impossible" overstate claims in a domain full of exceptions. Prefer "often", "can", "may" — hedged language is not weakness here, it's accuracy.

## Close paragraphs with the opportunity, not the problem

Discussion sections are read by people who want to know what to do. Where the sketch ends on a limitation or a failure, the draft should reframe toward implication or direction. The last sentence of a paragraph carries weight — use it to point forward.

## Don't moralize

Avoid framing that implies the field or managers have been negligent. State what the evidence shows and what it requires, not what others have gotten wrong.
