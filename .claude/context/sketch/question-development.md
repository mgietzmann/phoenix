# Question Development

Question Development is a focused step. The goal is to produce one well-formed research question that, when answered, will put meaningful material into the sketch.

Good questions are hard. A vague or poorly aimed question leads to unfocused searching and mining. This step deserves real effort.

---

## What You're Doing

You are helping the user identify a gap in the sketch and turn it into a precise, answerable research question. Work through three phases: Orient, Sharpen, Situate. The process is iterative — as the question evolves, revisit earlier phases if needed.

---

## Finding the Gap

Start by reading `Sketch.md` and `Questions.md`. Note which topic sentences have little or no development, and which questions have already been answered so you don't retread ground.

Then ask:
- Which part of the sketch feels most underdeveloped or uncertain right now?
- Is there a claim being made that has no support yet?
- Is there a section where you know roughly what you want to say but don't have the material to say it?

Once a gap is identified, get a rough version of the question from the user — however unformed — and then work through the phases below.

---

## Phase 1: Orient

Establish the context the question lives in. Without this, sharpening the question is working in a vacuum.

**Audience**
- Who is the primary reader? What field or background?
- What would they already accept as true going in?
- What would they be skeptical of?

**Purpose**
- If you answered this question perfectly, what argument does it support?
- What would you do with a "yes"? With a "no"?
- Is there a broader claim this question is meant to underpin?

> If the audience or purpose shifts later, return here before re-examining Sharpen.

---

## Phase 2: Sharpen

Interrogate the question itself. These tend to cascade — fixing one often surfaces the next problem — so treat this as a conversation, not a checklist.

**Specificity**
- Can any vague terms be replaced with something more concrete — a unit, a scale, a named thing?
- Is the question so specific it only applies to one case and stops generalizing?

**Falsifiability**
- What data or evidence would answer this?
- If you gathered that evidence, would the question actually be resolved?
- Is there any version of an answer that simply couldn't be obtained?

**Essentiality**
- If the answer came back as X, does your purpose follow? Or could someone accept the answer and still reject your purpose?
- Is there a more direct question that gets at the same thing?

**Causal depth**
- Are you asking what happens or why it happens?
- What picture needs painting so the reader and you are on the same page?
- What cause or mechanism needs to be established for the argument to hold?

---

## Phase 3: Situate

Zoom out. Now that the question has some shape, see how it fits with others.

**Sufficiency**
- If you had a perfect answer to this question, what would you still need to know?
- List the follow-up questions — can any be absorbed into this one with better framing?

**Related questions**
- What questions does this one depend on?
- What questions does it open up?

**Sequencing**
- What does a reader need to accept before this question makes sense?
- Are there prerequisite questions that need to come first?
- Where does this question sit in the larger argument?

---

## Outcome

A single, well-formed question ready to take into Material Gathering.

Update `Questions.md` by adding the new question under an `## Active` section (create it if it doesn't exist). Answered questions live under `## Answered` with a brief note on what was found.

Update `Quest.md`:
- Set the `Next:` line: `Next: Material Gathering — [the question, briefly]`
- Append a one-line summary to the `## Log` section (e.g. `- Question Development: developed Q5 (HMS migration distances) through illustration framing`)

---

## Guidance

- Take one phase at a time. Don't overwhelm the user with all questions at once — pick the most important ones and work back and forth until satisfied before moving on.
- Push back gently when answers are vague. The goal is a question that is specific, answerable, essential, and situated — not just a tidier version of what they started with.
- When the user seems stuck, make suggestions to get thinking moving. Offer options, not conclusions.
- One question at a time. It's tempting to generate a list — resist it. A single focused question leads to better material than a sprawling list leads to scattered material.
- The question should feel like it matters. If the user isn't genuinely curious about the answer, it's probably the wrong question.
- Keep the sketch in view throughout. The question exists to serve the sketch, not the other way around.
