# Guidelines: Introduction

An introduction builds a funnel — it opens on the broad context and progressively narrows to the specific problem the paper addresses, ending with a clear statement of aims. Every paragraph is a step in that narrowing.

---

## Structure

### The funnel
- Open at the level of the field or phenomenon, not the paper. The first paragraph orients the reader to the world they're entering.
- Each subsequent paragraph should narrow the frame: from ecosystem to food web to prey to predator challenge, or whatever the paper's specific logic demands.
- The final paragraph is the aims paragraph. It should follow naturally as the inevitable conclusion of everything that came before: the reader should feel the paper is the only sensible next step.

### Paragraph logic
- Each paragraph makes one claim (the lead). The development must support that claim — not simply list related facts, but build an argument for it.
- The final sentence of the development should resolve the paragraph: a mini-conclusion, or a "so what" that creates forward momentum into the next paragraph.
- Never end a development section on a citation alone. End on the implication.

### Transitions
- The opening of each paragraph should connect — explicitly or implicitly — to where the previous paragraph landed. The reader should never feel a jump.
- Avoid mechanical transitions ("Furthermore", "In addition", "Moreover"). Let the logic carry the reader forward. If the logic is clear, these words are not needed.
- If a lead needs revision for flow, the revision should preserve the original claim but adjust its framing to receive the prior paragraph and set up the next.

---

## Prose style

### Sentences
- Leads are declarative commitments. No hedging ("may", "might", "could") in a lead sentence unless uncertainty is itself the claim being made.
- Vary sentence length. Short sentences after a complex one give the reader a breath. Long sentences build accumulation and momentum.
- **Agent before effect.** Whatever is doing the acting — whether a named subject or a condition — comes first; what is acted upon comes second. This applies to both sentence structure ("solar heating warms the surface layer", not "the surface layer is warmed by solar heating") and causal logic ("where these forces converge, productivity emerges", not "productivity emerges where these forces converge"). Cause first, effect second.
- Prefer active voice, but don't strain for it. Passive is acceptable when the subject is unknown or unimportant.

### Development prose
- Do not restate the lead in the development. The lead made the claim; the development proves it.
- Use specific numbers, examples, and names where the sketch provides them — they are more persuasive than generalities.
- Build through the bullet points in order unless a different order reads better; if you reorder, make sure no fact is lost.
- Parallel structure for lists: if three things are related, make them grammatically parallel.
- **Scope claims to what the sketch supports.** If a process or pattern applies in some conditions or locations, say so. "Upwelling along coastlines" is too broad if the sketch doesn't claim it's universal; "along some coastlines" is not weaker writing — it is more accurate writing. Scrutinise any implied universality.
- **Avoid analogies not in the sketch.** If the sketch doesn't make a comparison, don't introduce one. Analogies that seem illustrative may be inaccurate or misleading in ways the draft author cannot catch. Only use comparisons directly supported by the source material.

### Citations
- Keep citations exactly as they appear in the sketch (e.g. `(Lalli, 2006)`).
- Embed citations at the end of the clause or sentence they support, not mid-sentence where they interrupt flow.
- When multiple points share the same citation, don't repeat it on every sentence — carry it once at the end of the run if it's clearly applicable throughout.

---

## The aims paragraph

- The aims paragraph is the exception to "development as prose": it may use a light list-like structure if the aims are enumerable (e.g. "This paper does three things: ..."), but the lead must still be a full sentence stating the paper's purpose.
- The aims should follow from what came before. If the reader has been taken on the right journey, the aims paragraph should feel inevitable.
- Do not inflate the aims. State exactly what the paper does, using the language of the sketch.
- **Write the aims as a sequential path, not a parallel list.** Use connectors that show forward movement: "We begin by... From there... We then..." This gives the reader a sense of traveling through the paper rather than scanning a table of contents.
- **Frame research opportunities positively.** Don't describe absent work as a gap, failure, or deficiency in the field. Frame it as an opportunity or an avenue yet to be fully explored. This is more accurate (absence of work is not a fault) and more motivating for the reader.

---

## What to watch for

- **Stacking facts without argument**: a development section that lists supporting data but never synthesises them into the claim is not finished prose. Find the thread that ties the facts together and make it explicit.
- **The loose final sentence**: the last sentence of a development often just recapitulates the last bullet point. Push it further — what does all of this *mean* for what comes next? Also check that the implied logic is accurate: "the concentrations move" implies temporal continuity; "the concentrations are ephemeral" implies independent appearance and disappearance. These are different claims — use the one the science supports.
- **Over-citing**: a sentence ending in three citations usually means three facts were compressed into one sentence without being integrated. Slow down and let them breathe.
- **Tonal flatness**: an introduction should have energy. The reader should feel the world being described is interesting and that the paper matters.
- **Implied absolutes**: language that implies a category is total, exhaustive, or universal ("the highly migratory species", "all coastlines", "the only explanation") overstates what the sketch supports and invites correction. Prefer partitive or scoped constructions ("a set of highly migratory species", "some coastlines") when the sketch does not assert universality.
