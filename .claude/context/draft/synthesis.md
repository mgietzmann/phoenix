# Synthesis

Synthesis is the expansive step. The goal is not to write — it's to think about what the piece is trying to do and whether its current structure is the right vehicle to do it.

This step runs at the start of every loop. It is also the step you return to after Mining has added new material to the sketch.

---

## Sketch Format

`Sketch.md` uses the following format throughout:

```
**Lead:** [topic sentence — a single, concrete claim]
**Development:**
- [development sentence]
- [development sentence]
```

Each `**Lead:**` is a paragraph commitment. `**Development:**` bullets are the supporting material that will eventually be fleshed into prose. When creating or editing `Sketch.md`, always use this format.

---

## What You're Doing

You are helping the user step back from the sketch and ask: does this structure still serve the conveyance?

"Conveyance" means: what is the piece trying to get the reader to understand, believe, or feel — and in what sequence does it need to take them there?

The sketch is a list of topic sentences (paragraph headers). These are the commitments the piece is making, in order. Synthesis is about interrogating those commitments.

---

## Cold Start — No Sketch Yet

If `Sketch.md` doesn't exist, don't try to build one in the session. The raw material needs to come from the user first.

Recommend they go for a walk — unrecorded, unrushed — and think deeply about what they're trying to say. The questions to carry on the walk:

- What's the one thing I want the reader to walk away believing?
- What's the journey I want to take them on to get there?
- Where does it start? Where does it land?

Ask them to come back with a seed: a rough transcript or notes — however messy — capturing the idea as it emerged. That seed becomes the raw material for the first Synthesis session.

Once they return with a seed, read it carefully and then work with them to draw out `**Lead:**` sentences that capture the shape of the argument. Build the initial `Sketch.md` together from there.

---

## How to Run This Step

Start by reading `Sketch.md` carefully. Absorb the current structure before saying anything.

Then open a conversation with the user. The aim is to surface:

**On the conveyance:**
- What is the piece ultimately trying to land? Is that still what the user wants?
- Does the sequence of topic sentences take the reader there logically?
- Are there gaps — places where the argument leaps without a bridge?
- Are there redundancies — topic sentences doing the same job?
- Is the opening doing the right work? Is it at the right level of abstraction?
- Is the ending a real landing, or does it just stop?

**On the individual topic sentences:**
- Does each one make a claim, or is it just a label?
- Are any too broad to be useful? Too narrow to carry a paragraph?
- Are they in the right order, or would a different sequence be more persuasive?

---

## Outcome

Topic sentences in `Sketch.md` may be added, removed, revised, or reordered. Development sentences already under a topic sentence should not be disturbed unless the topic sentence itself changes substantially.

When the user is satisfied with the structure, update `Sketch.md` and then update `Quest.md`:

- Set the `Next:` line (e.g. `Next: Question Development`)
- Append a one-line summary to the `## Log` section (e.g. `- Synthesis: reordered paragraphs 3–5; sharpened lead on predator challenge`)
- If the user feels the sketch is fully developed, note that and let them decide what comes next.

---

## Guidance

- This step can feel uncomfortable because it might undo work. That's okay — it's the point.
- Don't let the user rush through it. A well-structured sketch makes everything downstream easier.
- If the user isn't sure what they're trying to convey, that's the conversation to have before touching the sketch.
- Suggest specific revisions to topic sentences rather than abstract advice. Make it concrete.
- It's fine if this step is short — sometimes the structure is right and just needs minor tuning.
